How to read this page

These notes appear to span the practical parts of building a strong general-purpose LLM after base pretraining: what to optimize in post-training, how to evaluate the model, how to improve reasoning, how to preserve instruction-following and multilingual quality, and how serving constraints like chat templates and inference engines can quietly affect outcomes.

The handwritten source material is noisy, so the right move is not a perfect transcription fetish. The right move is to convert the notes into a durable mental model: which capabilities matter, how they interact, and what a sane implementation sequence looks like.

Interpretation rule: wherever the original notes were ambiguous, this page chooses a conceptually coherent framing that matches common LLM training practice rather than pretending every token was legible.

1. Post-training priorities

A recurring theme in the notes is that the most meaningful product behavior often emerges during post-training, not just during base pretraining. The question is less “did the model memorize more internet text?” and more “what qualities actually correlate with the outputs people care about?”

What matters most

  • Reasoning reliability on hard tasks
  • Instruction following and refusal/control behavior
  • Function calling / tool-use accuracy
  • Response quality and conversational coherence
  • Multilingual retention

What can go wrong

  • Over-optimizing for one benchmark while hurting broad utility
  • Reasoning improvements degrading style or latency
  • Data contamination inflating confidence in evals
  • Chat-template mismatches causing regressions at serving time

Post-training should be treated as a multi-objective optimization problem: gains in reasoning, style, tools, safety, and multilingual ability need balancing rather than isolated wins.

2. Evaluation stack

The notes reference a cluster of evals that together imply a broad evaluation philosophy: use multiple views of model quality rather than trusting a single score.

Benchmarks explicitly or implicitly referenced

  • Arena-Hard or arena-style preference evaluations for strong model-vs-model comparison
  • GPQA / OLMES-style hard knowledge and reasoning evaluations
  • MATH or math-heavy structured reasoning benchmarks
  • Pass@k thinking for smaller or stochastic task sets

Why this matters

  • Preference-based evals capture real-world helpfulness better than narrow exact-match metrics alone
  • Hard reasoning evals expose whether RL or chain-of-thought improvements are real
  • Specialized task evals catch regressions hidden by aggregate averages
Recommendation: split evals into capability buckets — reasoning, instruction following, tool use, multilingual, long-context, and response quality — and track each bucket separately over training runs.

3. Reasoning and reinforcement

Several note fragments point toward reasoning-focused post-training, likely referencing GRM-like or RL-driven approaches, DeepSeek-R1-style ideas, and the distinction between pure RL discovery versus a supervised cold start.

Core ideas

  • Reasoning can be improved after pretraining with targeted supervised or reinforcement signals.
  • Cold-start supervised data can stabilize downstream RL by giving the model a usable reasoning prior.
  • Pure zero-style RL approaches may surface strong behavior but can be unstable, weirdly formatted, or hard to control.
  • Reward design matters: optimizing only for final answer correctness can produce brittle or hacked behavior.

Likely note themes

  • GRM / reward-model or verifier-assisted optimization
  • R1-Zero vs supervised warm start
  • Reasoning quality versus readability trade-offs
  • Small-set evaluation via pass@k

Practical takeaway

  • Use a supervised seed before aggressive RL
  • Evaluate both correctness and answer usability
  • Watch for style collapse, verbosity drift, or tool-call breakage

4. Instruction following and chat quality

The source notes seem to treat instruction following as a first-class capability, not a side effect. That is the correct instinct. Many otherwise strong models fail in deployment because they reason well but obey poorly, ramble, misformat, or fail to preserve user intent.

Key goals

  • Follow explicit constraints without over-refusing
  • Generate answers in the expected shape, tone, and schema
  • Preserve high-quality conversational writing after reasoning optimization
  • Improve “oral” or natural-language fluency where outputs feel stiff or synthetic

This suggests a training mix that includes both instruction-tuning data and preference signals about response quality, brevity, directness, and usefulness.

5. Tool use and function calling

Tool use is called out explicitly in the notes, which is a good sign: function calling often regresses when teams chase reasoning gains without maintaining structured-action behavior.

  • Train tool selection and argument formatting explicitly
  • Evaluate exact schema adherence, not just semantic intent
  • Test tool behavior under multi-turn context, ambiguity, and partial information
  • Protect tool reliability during RL and style tuning
If a model becomes “smarter” but starts emitting malformed calls, the user experience still got worse.

6. Multilingual capability

Multilingual performance appears in the notes as a distinct concern, which usually means the author noticed a real pattern: post-training can easily narrow a model into English-centric chat behavior unless multilingual examples are intentionally preserved.

  • Keep multilingual supervised data in the post-training mixture
  • Benchmark across representative languages, not just translated English tasks
  • Check whether reward models or preference data are implicitly English-biased

7. Long-context and recall

Another visible topic is long-context handling — likely around recall, instruction persistence, and performance degradation over longer prompts. This area deserves its own eval bucket because many models look strong in short contexts but fail once retrieval burden and distraction increase.

  • Measure retrieval fidelity, not just max context window size
  • Test instruction persistence deep into context
  • Separate “needle retrieval” from actual synthesis across long documents

8. Data quality, contamination, and mixing

Some of the clearest operational notes concern data curation: decontamination, data mixing, smaller datasets, and supervised seeds. That cluster matters because post-training quality is heavily determined by what you mix, what you exclude, and how cleanly your evals are isolated from training exposure.

Data system principles

  • Decontaminate aggressively for benchmark integrity
  • Track mixture weights for reasoning, chat, tools, multilingual, and safety data
  • Use smaller high-quality sets carefully and evaluate with pass@k where stochasticity matters
  • Prefer explicit dataset taxonomies over giant undifferentiated training blobs

Good questions to ask

  • Which data bucket caused the gain?
  • Which capability regressed?
  • Is the win real or contamination-driven?

Good artifacts to keep

  • Mixture manifests
  • Benchmark exclusion lists
  • Per-capability scorecards
  • Template and tokenizer compatibility notes

9. Serving-time details: chat templates and inference stacks

The notes mention chat templates and vLLM compatibility, which is exactly the sort of “small” detail that causes surprisingly large regressions. A good model can look broken when the serving template differs from the training template, or when stop tokens and tool-call wrappers are inconsistent.

  • Keep training and inference chat templates aligned
  • Validate tool-call formatting under the actual serving stack
  • Test with vLLM or your target runtime before declaring a run successful
  • Document tokenizer, BOS/EOS, and special-token assumptions
Rule of thumb: model quality is not just weights + data; it is weights + data + template + runtime.

10. Suggested implementation roadmap

If these notes are meant to drive a real training effort, the cleanest execution path is a staged one.

  1. Define capability buckets: reasoning, chat, tools, multilingual, long-context, safety.
  2. Build the eval suite first: Arena-Hard-style preference evals, hard reasoning sets, tool-use tests, multilingual checks, long-context checks.
  3. Create a supervised post-training seed with strong formatting, tool usage, and instruction-following examples.
  4. Add reasoning optimization via verifier/reward-model/RL methods, while tracking regressions in other buckets.
  5. Decontaminate and rebalance if wins look suspicious or narrow.
  6. Validate in the real serving stack with the exact chat template and inference engine intended for deployment.

That roadmap matches the spirit of the notes: don’t chase a single magic trick. Build a system where reasoning, usefulness, and deployment reliability improve together.
